International Relations

The International System refers to the pattern of interactions between states with causal impact. The patterns of the system will influence how the actors within the system will behave, so studying the international system is practical and useful. By studying the system, we can determine how states will react to each other. For example, how the USSR will react when the US arms the South Vietnamese.

Forms of Security

Security is the absence of a threat to stability.
International security refers to the maintaining of the stability of the international system.
National security refers to the maintaining of the stability of countries.
Human security refers to the maintaining of the stability of individuals (i.e. standards of living).

Old ideas of security emphasized threats to and from states. States were thought of as autonomous, single-minded states with clear national interests. They employed military force to deter and respond to aggression.
Modern ideas of security emphasize that traditional military force is often inadequeate and less influential than soft power because States are subjected to institutional and social influence. Threats can also come from a variety of non-state actors.

Terrorism

Terrorism generally involves non-state actors who pursue political aims through cultivating fear and uncertainty. This is usually achieved through violence.

Power in International Relations

Military Dimension

Refers to the military preparedness of a nation. Military power is still important, although not always usable and sometimes counterproductive.

Economic Dimension

Refers to the economic strength and stability of a nation. The economic dimension of power supports the military dimension of power because you cannot finance a military without an economy. Some consider the economy to be a component of military power.
Economic power is a better fit for the era of globalization and interdependence than military power.

Military Dimension

Refers to the military preparedness of a nation. Military power is still important, although not always usable and sometimes counterproductive.

Structural Power

Refers to power over processes (ex. the process of decision making). Regimes and institutions are the chess pieces of structural power. The United States has enormous structural power through the foundation and popularity of the United Nations because it spreads American ideas and controls what is relevant by determining what gets discussed. This is extremely important.

Diplomatic Power

Also known as Soft Power. Rather than coercion, soft power spreads and strengthens via culture and legitimacy of values.

Major approaches to international security

Realism

Realism is a power politics model emphasizing the anarchy of the international sphere of politics, given the absence of a world government which can enforce an order. To a follwer of Realism, politics is all about a nonstop pursuit of power. Politics concerns egoism and self-interest. Therefore, the National Interest is emphasized over mutual interests. The state does not pursue cooperation and altruism. The realist tends to think that a state knows its national interests.
Additionally, Realism emphasizes conflict in the international sphere. Conflict is natural in a system of anarchy and egoism. Therefore, there is an interest in the pursuit of a balance of power in order to prevent unending war. States deter war by forming alliances, etc.
Above all, Realism is the belief that there are no systematic features to international relations.

Neorealism

Neorealism is a reformed version of Realism (hence the Neo). Neorealism is a structural model. It still emphasizes anarchy, but Neorealists realize there are recognizeable patterns in the international sphere. There is Anarchy, National Security, a Distribution of Capabilities, an International System, and a Polarity in power.
According to a Neorealist, states organize themselves in similar ways and perform similar functions. Nations in the domestic context are able to specialize in different sectors and provide security and order. However, in the international context there is only anarchy and nations can’t specialize in different things.
Distribution of capabilities: States are formally equal, but practically unequal. Therefore, States can be ranked in order of capability. For example, the US is the number one military power, followed by this country, that country, etc. This gives a sort of ranking to states which leads to an International System. According to a Neorealist, the International System is a structural system based on the capabilities of states, giving a sort of stability in the international order for short to medium term.
The rankings determine which polarity the system is currently running on. The polarity of power in the world determines much of the interactions in the international sphere.

Polarity in power

Unipolarity: One state with greatly disproportionate share of power. One state is way ahead of the others. There are no major rivals. This is the system from 1991-Early 2000’s that emerged with the collapse of the Soviet Union.
Bipolarity: Two major powers. Involves a Zero-Sum Competition (any gains made by the one side is a direct loss to the other). The Cold War is demonstrative of a Bipolar international order, with the USA and USSR being the two powers competing for global domination.
Multipolarity: More than two major powers. Having a multipolar power system encourages a balancing behavior. The poles do not want power concentrated too heavily in one of the powers. But in this international order there is enormous potential for lethal mistakes. There is less clarity/certainty in the consequences of actions, so countries could step too far or overreact and make a miscalculation leading to conflict. This is the system which dominated continental Europe from 15th-20th century.

Liberalism

Liberalism is an interdependence model emphasizing cooperation and collective security in the international sphere.
Liberalism believes that International regimes are ways in which cooperation have emerged (i.e. through the UN). International regimes are a set of rules and norms that constrain – usually through norms and social condemnation. Nations don't go to war because of the social consequences for doing so.
Liberals (in the context of international relations) are quick to give the domestic analogy. They believe that international politics isn’t very different from domestic politics. They are analagous. If the domestic sphere began with anarchy but has formed systems of order and cooperation, then the international sphere can too. Liberals are also quick to give the Democratic Peace Thesis by Steven Pinker. The theory states that democracies do not tend to fight each other, which is the reason for such a long period of relative peace. This gives the idea that State behavior is defined by what kind of State you are.

Constructivism

Constructivism is a model emphasizing the role of norms in mediating state interations. Shared ideals and implicit rules can serve as structures for an international order. The view is best summarized by Alexander Wendt: “Anarchy is what states make of it.”

Feminism

Feminism is a model critiquing masculine biases, emphasizing that the other schools of international thought miss that there’s more to fix than fear and competition (i.e. the importance of aid to women, need to compensate for gender inequality).
Feminist international thinkers have a theory called The Security Paradox. Realists act as if anarchy requires certain outcomes (fear, conflict, etc.) but this is a male approach, and the maleness of the international sphere is responsible for conflict. Defending your own security interests may lead you to take actions that actually make you more venerable. Gender is a construct, is artificial, and is imposed on international relations.

Authority
Authority is legitimate power. Where power is the ability to influence others, Authority is the right. Authority is based on an acknowledged duty to obey rather than on any form of coercion or manipulation. Weber distinguishes three types of Authority: Traditional authority is based on history, Charismatic authority is based on personality, and Legal-Rational authority is based on a set of universal rules.
